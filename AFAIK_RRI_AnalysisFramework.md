## PHASE 2: CONTENT CAPTURE

### 2A. Explicit Content
**What is directly taught?**
- Main claims, definitions, frameworks, theories
- Historical/factual information presented
- Examples, case studies, illustrations
- Stated learning objectives or takeaways

### 2B. Implicit Assumptions
**What is presupposed or taken for granted?**
- Underlying values, worldviews, ontologies
- Unstated norms (e.g., "normal" family, "standard" development, "rational" actors)
- Who is the imagined audience? Whose experiences are centered?
- What counts as "knowledge" or "evidence"?
- Temporal, spatial, and cultural defaults (e.g., linear time, Western institutions, individualism)

### 2C. Discipline-Specific Observations
**Field-relevant analytical notes:**
- For **Humanities:** Canon assumptions, interpretive authority, whose voices are validated?
- For **Social Sciences:** Positionality of researchers/authors, power dynamics, sampling/representation?
- For **Natural Sciences:** Methods, reproducibility, uncertainty communication, environmental impact?
- For **Technical Sciences:** Supply chains, who benefits/suffers, accessibility, automation bias?
- For **Mathematics:** Axioms stated or hidden? Historical credit? Multiple solution paths shown?
- For **Medicine:** Whose bodies are normative? Biomedical vs. social model? Informed consent?

## PHASE 4: STRENGTHS & WEAKNESSES

### 4A. Strengths
- **Clarity & Accessibility:** Is the brick easy to understand? For whom?
- **Practical Utility:** Does it prepare learners for real-world application?
- **Inclusivity:** Which perspectives/populations are well-represented?
- **Rigor & Evidence:** Is the foundation solid and current?
- **Pedagogical Design:** Are diverse learning styles/abilities supported?

### 4B. Weaknesses
- **Exclusions & Erasures:** Who is missing? What alternatives are unseen?
- **Problematic Assumptions:** What normative claims are presented as universal?
- **Risk of Misuse:** Could this reinforce stereotypes, justify oppression, or harm vulnerable groups?
- **Outdated/Incomplete Evidence:** Are contemporary critiques, intersectional analyses, or decolonial work missing?
- **Accessibility Barriers:** Language complexity, examples, formats‚Äîfor whom does this exclude?

---

## PHASE 5: EVIDENCE, SCOPE & TIMELINESS

**Assess the quality and currency of the brick's foundation:**

- **Foundation:** What scholarly/empirical basis exists? Is it cited? Peer-reviewed?
- **Currency:** How recent are sources? Are contemporary critiques, decolonial scholars, intersectional perspectives included?
- **Complexity:** Appropriately pitched for target audience? Over/under-simplified?
- **Depth/Breadth:** Comprehensive coverage of field? Or narrow/canonical?
- **Contextualization:** Is the brick embedded in historical, geographic, cultural, political context? Or presented as "timeless/universal"?
- **Reproducibility/Transferability:** Can findings/claims be verified? Do they apply across contexts or are they locally contingent?

### 6B. Field-Specific How-To Examples

**HUMANITIES:**  
"Integrate non-Western ethics (Ubuntu, Confucianism, Islamic) as *equals* to Western analytic tradition, not exotic variants. Reframe as 'Multiple Moral Epistemologies' not 'Western vs. Other.' Credit non-Western philosophers equally; show their internal coherence, not just 'comparison.'"

**SOCIAL SCIENCES:**  
"Add section: 'Whose Positionality?' Reflect on researcher/author identity (race, gender, class, nationality, institutional power). Name positionality; acknowledge blind spots. Include voices from the population studied, not just external analysis."

**NATURAL SCIENCES:**  
"Communicate uncertainty probabilistically, not as fact. Example: Not 'Genes cause X' but 'influence P(X) by Y% under conditions Z.' Explicitly state limits of methods, sample size, confounding factors, reproducibility challenges."

**TECHNICAL SCIENCES:**  
"Analyze lifecycle: Material extraction ‚Üí Manufacturing ‚Üí Data collection/processing ‚Üí Use ‚Üí Waste/Disposal. For each stage: Who suffers environmental damage? Whose data is harvested? What surveillance happens? Who profits?"

**MATHEMATICS:**  
"Acknowledge axioms explicitly. Credit all mathematicians (Al-Khwarizmi, Emmy Noether, Ramanujan, etc.), not just Euler/Gauss. Show multiple proofs or solution methods. Name historical erasures; invite alternative approaches."

**MEDICINE:**  
"Examine whose bodies are normative. Add: social determinants of health, accessibility/disability justice, informed consent challenges, environmental health impacts. Name pharma colonialism; include non-biomedical healing traditions."

## NOTES FOR ANALYSTS

1. **Be Specific, Not Generic:** Every statement should reference the actual brick content, not abstract principles. Use direct quotes when possible.

2. **Concrete Examples:** For every bias or gap, provide a concrete example from the brick or propose a specific fix, not vague suggestions.

3. **Prioritize Ruthlessly:** Not all gaps are equally important. Equity-harming biases (e.g., perpetuating stereotypes, erasing marginalized knowledge) rank highest.

4. **Fair Balance:** Criticize with evidence and nuance. Acknowledge genuine strengths; don't dismiss entire bricks as "bad." Show appreciation for what works, then show where it can improve.

5. **Realistic Effort Estimates:** Be honest. Adding diverse perspectives ‚â† surface edits. Structural changes take time.

6. **Invite Collaboration:** Analysis should suggest who should be consulted (scholars from marginalized fields, affected communities, etc.) to make improvements authentic, not extractive.
